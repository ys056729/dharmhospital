Cataract (PHACO)

Advanced micro-incision surgery with premium foldable & multifocal lens implants for rapid recovery.

Stitchless & Painless

Phacoemulsification ("phaco") is the gold-standard, minimally invasive cataract surgery using ultrasonic energy to emulsify and remove a cloudy lens through a tiny 2–3 mm incision.

    Key Aspects of Phaco Surgery:


  • Procedure: A small incision is made, and the surgeon uses a phaco probe to break down the cataract with ultrasound and aspirate the pieces.

  • Techniques: Common techniques include phaco chop (holding the nucleus with high vacuum to divide it) and four-quadrant splitting.

  • Limitations: May not be suitable for extremely advanced/hard cataracts or patients with severe weak zonules.

  • Advantages: Lower complication rates, astigmatism management, and faster visual rehabilitation.

  • Recovery: Most patients resume normal activities in 2-3 days, with complete healing over 4–6 weeks.

LASIK Laser

Say goodbye to glasses forever with world-class laser vision correction technology and precision.

Sharp Vision

LASIK (Laser-Assisted in Situ Keratomileusis) is a popular, 10–15 minute painless surgery that uses lasers to reshape the cornea to correct myopia (nearsightedness), hyperopia (farsightedness), and astigmatism.

    Key Details About LASIK Laser Surgery:


  • Procedure: A, microkeratome or femtosecond laser creates a thin flap on the cornea. A computer-controlled excimer laser then reshapes the cornea to improve focusing, typically lasting less than 60 seconds.

  • Benefits: Over 99% of patients achieve 20/40 vision or better, with many achieving 20/20 or better. It offers fast recovery, with significant vision improvement within 24 hours.

  • Risks & Side Effects: Temporary side effects include dry eyes, glare, halos, and light sensitivity. Rare complications can occur.

  • Recovery: Initial recovery takes about 24 hours, with full stabilization usually within 3-6 months.

Retina & Glaucoma

Comprehensive diagnosis and advanced management for retinal diseases and intraocular pressure.

Expert Care

What is glaucoma? by Dr. Arpita Gupta – WebvisionGlaucoma and retinal diseases often intersect, as both affect the optic nerve and retinal ganglion cells at the back of the eye, leading to potential irreversible vision loss.

    Key Connections Between Retina & Glaucoma:


  • Retinal Nerve Fiber Layer (RNFL) Thinning: Glaucoma is characterized by the progressive degeneration of retinal ganglion cells, resulting in a measurable thinning of the RNFL.

  • Vascular Issues: Reduced blood flow to the retina (vascular dysregulation) is a key factor in the development of glaucoma, particularly in normal-tension glaucoma where pressure is not high.

  • Concurrent Conditions: Retinal diseases like diabetic retinopathy can coexist with glaucoma, making management complex and increasing the risk of blindness.

  • Imaging & Diagnosis: Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT) is crucial to assess both retinal health and nerve fiber layer thickness, providing detailed structural information for diagnosis.
